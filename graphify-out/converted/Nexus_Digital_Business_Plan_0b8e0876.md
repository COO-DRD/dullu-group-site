# Founder's Note — The Honest Starting Point
This business was designed for a specific founder: 19 years old, based in Kilifi/Mombasa, strong background in design and marketing, zero starting capital, network of friends across finance, politics, personal branding, and household services. No ID yet. Just graduated.
This plan does not pretend otherwise. Every decision — which clients to target, which tools to use, which services to offer, how to price, how to grow — is made with that exact starting point in mind. The goal is not to build a startup. The goal is to build a cash-flowing, reputation-based premium service business that grows from one client to a sustainable operation within 12 months.
The model is simple: you are the Architect. You design the digital strategy, manage the client relationship, and delegate execution to vetted, accredited experts. You keep the margin between what clients pay and what experts charge. As you grow, you systematise. As you systematise, you scale.

# What's Inside


## 1.1 The Core Model Explained
Nexus Digital operates as a premium digital agency built on an Architect-Expert model. You — the founder — are the Architect: the single point of contact for the client, responsible for strategy, relationship management, quality control, and delivery oversight. The execution of each service is delegated to a curated network of vetted freelance experts — designers, developers, copywriters, SEO specialists, ads managers — who are paid a fixed rate. You charge the client a premium rate and keep the margin.


## 1.2 Why This Model Works With Zero Capital
- No stock, no office, no employees on payroll. Your only costs are tools — most of which are free at the start.
- Clients pay deposits upfront (50%). You pay experts after deposit is received. You never finance projects from your own pocket.
- Experts are paid per project, not monthly. This means zero fixed labour costs until you have revenue.
- Your value is not your execution — it is your judgment, taste, and relationships. Those cost nothing and are very hard to copy.

## 1.3 The Margin Logic
The fundamental principle: charge clients premium rates, pay experts competitive-but-fair project rates, keep the difference. The margin should be 40–60% of what you charge. At scale, this margin funds reinvestment into tools, team, and brand. In the early stage, it is your income.


## 2.1 Why This Specific Market
Politicians, executives, and public figures are the optimal first market for a premium digital agency for three specific reasons: they pay well without extended negotiation, they have urgent and ongoing digital needs, and a single well-delivered project opens an entire network through referrals. One governor's office client can cascade into county executives, party members, and business associates. One corporate executive client refers you to three peers in their network.
Critically for the Coast: Kenya's 2027 election cycle is already beginning to warm up. Political candidates at every level — gubernatorial, senatorial, parliamentary, ward — will spend aggressively on digital presence in 2025–2027. This is a once-every-5-years revenue surge and you are positioned to capture it early.

## 2.2 The Three Client Archetypes
### Archetype 1 — The Political Candidate / Elected Official
Profile: Aspiring or sitting politician at county or national level. Kilifi, Mombasa, Kwale counties are your geographic starting ground. Age 30–60. Urgent need to look credible, modern, and accessible. Often technologically unsophisticated personally but surrounded by advisors who understand the need for digital.
What they need: A strong personal brand website, active social media presence, WhatsApp broadcast strategy, voter-facing content, and during campaigns — aggressive paid digital advertising to specific constituencies.
What they pay: Political clients pay generously but often in tranches tied to campaign milestones. Expect Ksh 150,000–500,000+ per campaign cycle per candidate. Retainers of Ksh 50,000–120,000/month for ongoing digital management.

### Archetype 2 — The Corporate Executive / Business Leader
Profile: C-suite executive at a Mombasa or Nairobi company, MD of a Coast-based enterprise, or a high-profile entrepreneur. Age 35–55. Values discretion, quality, and results. Unlikely to engage on price. Will pay for a vendor who understands their world and communicates like a professional.
What they need: Executive personal brand website, LinkedIn strategy and content, media presence management, thought leadership content, and Google-optimised digital footprint. Some need their company's digital presence elevated alongside their personal brand.
What they pay: Ksh 80,000–250,000 for initial setup. Monthly retainers of Ksh 35,000–80,000 for ongoing management.

### Archetype 3 — The Emerging Public Figure
Profile: Pastor, media personality, influencer with real-world influence, motivational speaker, doctor/professional building a public presence. 25–45. Growing fast, knows they need digital infrastructure but hasn't had the right partner. Often willing to start smaller but become your most loyal long-term client.
What they pay: Ksh 40,000–120,000 for setup. Retainers of Ksh 20,000–45,000/month. Lower than the top two but faster to close and excellent referral sources within their communities.

## 4.2 The Vetting Process — Never Hire Blind
Your reputation is built on the quality of your experts' work. One bad delivery destroys a relationship that took months to build. The following vetting process is non-negotiable before you engage any expert on a client project:
- Request a portfolio of 3–5 completed projects in the relevant discipline. For designers, look at actual brand work. For developers, visit live websites. For SEO, ask for before/after ranking screenshots. For ads, ask for campaign results (impressions, clicks, conversion rates).
- Give them a paid micro-test. Before any client project, commission a small test task for Ksh 2,000–5,000: a one-page design concept, a landing page prototype, a sample SEO audit. This reveals their quality, communication style, and reliability far more than a portfolio.
- Assess communication speed and clarity. A great expert who responds 4 days later and communicates vaguely is a liability on a premium client project. You need experts who respond within 24 hours and can write a clear brief response.
- Have a direct rate conversation. Agree on: standard project rates, revision policy (how many rounds included), turnaround times, and payment terms (you pay on delivery or on milestone). Document this in writing via WhatsApp or email.
- Start with one small real project before giving them a major account. Build the relationship carefully.

## 4.3 Managing Experts — Your Internal SOP
Standard Operating Procedure for every expert engagement:
- Always brief in writing — a WhatsApp voice note is not a brief. Use the Brief Template (Section 8, Workflow 3).
- Set a deadline that is 3 days before your client deadline. Always build buffer.
- Review work before forwarding to client. You are the quality filter, not a pass-through.
- Never let the expert contact the client directly. You are the single interface. This protects your position and the relationship.
- Pay promptly. Experts who are paid reliably and on time will prioritise your work over others. This is a competitive advantage.
- Give feedback clearly and specifically. 'This isn't quite right' is not useful. 'The font is too informal for a political client — please use something more authoritative like Merriweather or Playfair' is useful.

## 5.2 Payment Terms — Critical for Cash Flow
- ALL projects: 50% deposit before work begins. No exceptions. This covers expert costs and protects you.
- Remaining 50%: on final delivery before files/access are transferred. Use this as leverage for timely client approvals.
- Monthly retainers: invoiced on the 25th of each month for the following month. Paid in advance.
- Political campaign clients: milestone-based. 30% at brief signing, 40% at campaign launch, 30% on completion.
- Late payment: add a clear 10-day payment window on all invoices. After 10 days, pause all work until payment is received. Communicate this policy upfront — it is professional, not aggressive.

## 5.3 The Discount Policy
You do not discount on price. You adjust on scope. If a client cannot afford the full package, reduce what is included — do not reduce your margin. Discounting signals that your prices were arbitrary to begin with. Scope adjustment signals professionalism and respect for your own value.

## 9.1 The Three Sales Channels
You have no marketing budget. You have three assets: your network, your skills, and your time. These three channels use all three assets and require zero money.
### Channel 1 — The Warm Network Offensive
This is your fastest path to the first Ksh 50,000. You have a friend in politics. You have contacts from school, design work, and your local community. Every one of them knows at least one person who is a politician, executive, or aspiring public figure.
- Write a list of every person you know personally. Next to each name, write what they do and who they might know.
- Identify the top 10 people most likely to know a politician, executive, or public figure.
- Have a direct, honest conversation with each of them: 'I have started a digital agency. I am building it for politicians and executives. If you know anyone who needs what I do, I will pay you a 10% introduction fee on any project that closes.'
- That 10% referral fee is your sales incentive. On a Ksh 120,000 project, your friend earns Ksh 12,000 for one introduction. This is real money for real people. Most will take it seriously.
### Channel 2 — The Free Digital Audit Offer
Identify 10 politicians or executives with weak digital presence. Research them: check their website (if they have one), their social media, their Google search result. Find 3 specific weaknesses.
Send a WhatsApp or email: 'I noticed your digital presence has some gaps that may be costing you [votes / visibility / leads]. I have prepared a free 1-page audit for you. Can I send it?'
Most people say yes to free. The audit is your proof of intelligence and your door-opener. It costs you 2 hours to prepare and regularly converts to a discovery call.
### Channel 3 — LinkedIn + Personal Brand Content
Post on LinkedIn 3 times per week. Topics that attract your specific clients:
- '5 digital mistakes Kenyan politicians make before an election' — political clients will share this.
- 'What does your Google search result say about you? A guide for executives' — executive clients will share this.
- 'I rebuilt a Coast-based business owner's digital presence in 21 days. Here's what changed.' — shows results, builds credibility.
You are not posting to get likes. You are posting to be found by the specific person who searches 'digital agency for politicians Kenya' or is referred to your profile by a mutual connection.

How a Client Goes From Lead to Delivered |

| 1. Lead Identified | A prospect is identified through your network, referral, or organic outreach. Log them immediately in HubSpot CRM with name, contact, source, and initial notes. |

| 3. Discovery Call | Scheduled via Calendly. Use the MCAT qualification framework from your masterclass. Take notes in Notion. Understand their problem deeply before mentioning services. |
| 4. Digital Audit | Within 48 hours of the discovery call, send a free one-page digital audit of their current presence. Use Canva template. This is your trust-builder and the bridge to the proposal. |
| 5. Proposal Sent | Custom proposal built in Canva, exported as PDF, sent via email with a Loom video walkthrough (2–3 minutes: 'Here is what I recommend and why'). Follow up in 48 hours. |
| 6. Proposal Discussion | Second call to walk through the proposal, answer questions, handle objections. Close with a specific decision question: 'Shall we begin with the deposit this week?' |
| 7. Deposit Invoice | Issue invoice via Wave Accounting. 50% of project fee. Share M-Pesa or bank details. Send payment reminder at 48 hours if unpaid. |
| 8. Deposit Received | Confirm receipt immediately via WhatsApp. Brief your experts the same day. Project officially begins. |
| Step | Action & Tool |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Client Brief | Record all client inputs in a Notion brief document: goals, audience, tone, competitors, references, content assets needed. |
| 2. Expert Brief | Write a specific, written brief for each expert using the Brief Template. Send via WhatsApp or email. Confirm they understand and set a deadline (3 days before your client deadline). |
| 3. Midpoint Check | At 50% of project timeline, check in with expert. Review work in progress. Give feedback early — do not wait until the final submission. |
| 4. Expert Delivery | Expert delivers work to you. Review against the brief and the client's stated goals. Check quality, accuracy, and professionalism before forwarding. |
| 5. Revision Round | If revisions needed, brief the expert clearly in writing. Include 1–2 revision rounds in your service. Beyond that, additional cost applies. |
| 6. Client Preview | Send the deliverable to the client with a Loom walkthrough video. Frame it as a preview: 'Here is the first version — please review and send me your feedback within 3 days.' |

| 8. Final Delivery + Invoice | Deliver final files. Issue balance invoice (50%). Transfer domain/access/files only on receipt of final payment. |
| 9. Pay Expert | Pay expert within 24 hours of receiving client's final payment. Reliability with experts is your competitive advantage. |
| 10. Post-Delivery Follow-Up | One week after delivery, check in: 'How is everything looking? Any questions?' This is also the moment to introduce the retainer conversation. |

| SECTION 12
Risk, Failure Points & How to Handle Them
What Will Go Wrong & What to Do |
| --- |
| Risk | How to Prevent & Handle It |
| --- | --- |
| Expert delivers bad work | Vet thoroughly first (Section 4.2). Always review before sending to client. Build buffer time into deadlines. If bad work is delivered, fix it before the client sees it — never pass the problem to the client. |
| Client doesn't pay | 50% deposit rule eliminates 80% of this risk. For the remaining 50%, send invoice immediately on delivery. Do not release files until paid. Enforce your payment terms professionally and consistently. |
| You take on too much too fast | Resist the temptation to take 5 clients in Month 2. Quality over volume. One badly delivered project costs more in reputation than one project's revenue. |
| Expert goes silent mid-project | Always have a backup expert for every discipline. Never be single-sourced on a critical role. Have two developers, two designers, etc. |
| Client wants changes beyond scope | A clear scope document signed before work begins prevents this. When scope creep happens, name it: 'That is outside our agreed scope — here is the additional cost to include it.' |
| You undercharge and resent it | Charge properly from Client 1. It is harder to raise prices on existing clients than to charge correctly from the start. Your instinct will be to discount to win — resist this completely. |
| You spend money before it arrives | Do not spend any project revenue until the final payment is received. Many projects stall between deposit and final payment. Treat deposit as non-spendable until delivery is complete. |
| Imposter syndrome stops you from outreaching | The honest truth: you will feel unqualified to call a politician's office or email an executive. Do it anyway. What is the worst they say? No. You are 19 in Kilifi and you have nothing to lose and everything to gain. |
