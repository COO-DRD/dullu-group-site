| Africa doesn't have a startup funding problem.

It has a monetisation architecture problem.

Unpopular opinion. Let me explain.

Between 2015 and 2024, African startups raised over $30 billion in venture capital.

And yet:
→ 70% of African startups fail within the first 3 years
→ Most VC-funded African startups have never reached profitability
→ The continent's largest "exits" have mostly been acqui-hires or write-downs

The narrative is always: "Africa needs more funding."

I think that's the wrong diagnosis.

What African startups need is better monetisation architecture.

Here's what I mean:

MONETISATION ARCHITECTURE is the set of systems, infrastructure,
and processes that turn a product or service into predictable, scalable revenue.

It includes:
→ Pricing structure (not just what you charge, but how)
→ Pipeline infrastructure (how you find and close buyers)
→ Retention systems (how you keep customers and expand revenue)
→ Payment infrastructure (how money actually moves from customer to you)

Most African startups are exceptionally good at building products.

They are systemically weak at building the architecture around the product
that turns it into a business.

I've seen this pattern repeatedly:

A fintech with a brilliant product and no go-to-market pipeline.
A logistics startup with strong unit economics and zero customer retention system.
An edtech with 10,000 free users and no monetisation path to even 1,000 paid ones.

The product is not the problem.

The architecture is.

And unlike venture capital — which requires investor relationships, pitch decks, and luck —
monetisation architecture is something you can build right now,
with the tools and resources you already have.

That's what I focus on.
That's what Dullu Group builds.

| The Nairobi founder playbook is broken.

Here's the new one.

THE OLD PLAYBOOK (and why it fails):

Step 1: Build a product
Step 2: Find investors
Step 3: Hire a team
Step 4: Figure out how to sell it

The problem: by the time most founders get to Step 4,
they've burned 18 months and someone else's money
discovering that their go-to-market is completely wrong.

I've watched brilliant founders follow this playbook
straight into a wall — not because they weren't talented,
but because the sequence was backwards.

THE NEW PLAYBOOK:

Step 1: Identify a specific, painful, and frequent problem
— Not "businesses need marketing." Something like:
"Mombasa logistics companies lose 30% of leads because
they have no system to respond to enquiries within 24 hours."

Step 2: Validate with a paying customer before building
— Find one person with that exact problem who will pay for the solution.
Not beta. Not free. Paying. This is your proof of concept.

Step 3: Build the minimum system that solves it
— Not the full product. The smallest version that delivers the result.

Step 4: Build the pipeline and retention infrastructure
— Now you systematise the finding, selling, and keeping of customers.

Step 5: Now scale
— With a proven problem, a paying customer base, and working systems,
you have something worth scaling — and something investors will actually fund.

The old playbook builds products.

The new playbook builds businesses.

There's a difference.

| I read 3 African tech failure post-mortems this week.

Different founders. Different industries. Different countries.

The same word appeared in all 3: "traction."

"We couldn't get traction."
"We ran out of runway before finding traction."
"We pivoted twice but still couldn't achieve traction."

Here's what they actually meant — and why the word is dangerous.

"Traction" is a vague word.

It sounds technical. It sounds like a startup metric.

But in every post-mortem I read, what the founder meant was:

"We didn't have a reliable, repeatable system for
finding people who would pay for what we built."

That is a pipeline problem.
Not a traction problem. Not a market problem.
A pipeline problem.

The word "traction" hides the real issue
because it sounds like a market verdict —
as if the market rejected the idea.

But most of the time, the market didn't reject anything.
The market never got a proper chance to respond.

Because the founder was broadcasting, not prospecting.
Posting, not pipeline-building.
Hoping to be discovered, rather than systematically reaching the people
who needed the product.

If you're reading this and you've used the word "traction"
to describe a problem in your business —

ask yourself this instead:

"Do I have a system that identifies my ideal customer,
reaches them with a relevant message,
qualifies them, and follows up automatically?"

If the answer is no — that's your traction problem.

And unlike "traction," that one has a solution.

"Let me start with what I actually observe — not what the headlines say.

African tech in 2026 is in a fascinating and complicated place.

On one hand: the infrastructure has genuinely improved.
Mobile penetration across East Africa is above 90% in urban areas.
Internet access costs have dropped significantly over the past 5 years.
Payment infrastructure — led by M-Pesa and its competitors —
has made digital transactions possible at a scale that most of the world
still can't replicate.

That is real. That is not theater.

On the other hand:

The 'African startup ecosystem' as a narrative
has significantly outrun the 'African startup ecosystem' as a reality.

What I mean by that: we have developed a very sophisticated vocabulary
around African innovation — hubs, accelerators, incubators,
pitch competitions, ecosystem reports —

and a much less sophisticated set of actual businesses
generating predictable revenue from African customers.

The scoreboard for African-built, African-serving technology businesses
that have reached genuine scale, genuine profitability, and genuine resilience —

that scoreboard is shorter than the conference circuit suggests."

SECTION 2: WHAT'S THEATER (3:00–6:30)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Let me be specific about what I mean by theater.

THEATER #1: The pitch competition ecosystem.

Africa now has hundreds of pitch competitions.
Regional, continental, diaspora-funded, NGO-sponsored.

Some are genuinely useful for visibility and connections.

But many have created a class of founder
whose primary skill is pitching —
not building, not selling, not operating.

I have met founders who have won 4 or 5 competitions
and have fewer than 20 paying customers.

Winning a pitch competition is not product-market fit.
It is performance. Sometimes very good performance.
But it is not the same thing.

THEATER #2: The 'Africa-focused fund' announcement cycle.

Every quarter there is a new fund announcement:
'$X million committed to African tech innovation.'

This is exciting. Some of it is real money.

But a fund announcement is not a deployed cheque.
A deployed cheque is not a company.
A company is not a business that generates returns.

The pipeline from 'fund announced' to 'founder actually building something
that works at scale' is much longer and more uncertain than the press releases suggest.

THEATER #3: Social media activity as business activity.

This one is uncomfortable because I am a content creator
and I benefit from the conflation.

But I want to be honest:

A large following is not a large business.
A viral post is not a growing pipeline.
100,000 impressions on LinkedIn is not 10 new clients.

Content builds awareness. Systems build revenue.
The theater is when founders — and agencies — treat them as the same thing.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SECTION 3: WHAT'S ACTUALLY WORKING (6:30–10:30)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Now here's the good news. And it is genuinely good.

WHAT'S WORKING #1: Infrastructure-first businesses.

The African companies that have scaled — genuinely scaled —
have almost all been infrastructure businesses.

Not apps. Infrastructure.

Safaricom and M-Pesa didn't win because of a great interface.
They won because they solved a genuine infrastructure problem —
the movement of money between people who didn't have bank accounts —
with a system that worked on the most basic mobile phone.

Equity Bank didn't scale because of marketing.
They scaled because they built the agent banking infrastructure
that brought banking to people the traditional banks couldn't reach.

The pattern: find the infrastructure gap,
build the system that fills it, and distribute it
at the right price point for the actual market.

Not the aspirational market. The actual market.

WHAT'S WORKING #2: B2B services and automation.

The fastest-growing segment of African tech that I observe
is not consumer apps. It's B2B services.

Companies that help other companies operate more efficiently.
Automation agencies. ERP implementation firms. Digital transformation consultancies.

Why? Because African businesses are at an inflection point.

They have reached a size where manual processes are breaking them —
but they cannot afford the enterprise software built for Fortune 500 companies.

The gap between 'Excel spreadsheet' and 'SAP implementation'
is enormous. And that gap is a market.

WHAT'S WORKING #3: Founders who build systems before they build audiences.

I keep coming back to this because I keep seeing it validated.

The founders who are growing most consistently right now —
in my observation, in my network, in the businesses I work with —
are the ones who built the operational infrastructure of their business first.

The pipeline. The delivery system. The retention mechanism.
Then they added the content and the audience.

Not the other way around."

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SECTION 4: WHERE THE REAL OPPORTUNITY IS (10:30–13:00)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"So where does this leave us as founders and operators in 2026?

Here's my honest read on the three biggest opportunities:

OPPORTUNITY 1: B2B automation for African mid-market companies.

These are companies with 20 to 200 employees.
Too big to operate on spreadsheets and WhatsApp.
Too small — or too capital-constrained — for enterprise software.

They need pipeline systems. CRM. Automation.
Custom-built for their actual workflow, not adapted from a US enterprise template.

This is a massive, underserved market.
And it requires a builder who understands African business context,
not just the tools.

OPPORTUNITY 2: Distribution infrastructure.

Whatever you are selling — whether it's a product or a service —
the founder who can build the most efficient distribution infrastructure
in their vertical will win.

Not the best product. The best distribution.

This is true globally, but it is especially true in Africa
where physical and digital distribution are still being built simultaneously.

OPPORTUNITY 3: Credibility infrastructure.

I'll be transparent about this one because it's what I'm building.

African founders have a credibility problem.
Not a talent problem. Not a knowledge problem.

A credibility infrastructure problem.

There is no robust, trustworthy, continent-native publishing infrastructure
for African business and tech knowledge.

No African equivalent of Harvard Business Review.
No African equivalent of First Round Review.
No African equivalent of Stratechery.

media.dullugroup.co.ke is my small, early contribution to filling that gap.

But the opportunity is enormous for anyone who can build
a credible, consistent, high-quality knowledge platform
for African founders and executives."
